Quick Fact

In print magazines, full-bleed images often extend to the page edges, creating a sense of infinity and visual power.

Asymmetrical layouts break the monotony of centered, symmetrical design. By placing content off-center, you create dynamic tension and draw the reader's eye in unexpected directions.

This technique was heavily used in magazines from the 1960s through 1990s. Swiss Style design embraced grid systems, but American and European magazines experimented with more chaotic, expressive arrangements.

The key is balance—even in asymmetrical layouts, visual weight must be carefully distributed. A large image on the left might be balanced by a column of text and smaller elements on the right.

Pro Tip

Use odd numbers of columns in your grid (3, 5, 7) to encourage asymmetrical layouts naturally.
