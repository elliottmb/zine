Elegant Simplicity
Less is more—a retrospective on minimalism

Minimalism never goes out of style. While maximalist designers fill every inch of space, minimalists understand the power of restraint.

A single typeface. A clean grid. Plenty of whitespace. One accent color, used sparingly. This approach builds elegance through subtraction.

The challenge: making simplicity feel intentional, not lazy. Every element must earn its place.

Pro Tip
Use line-height and margin to create rhythm in simple layouts. Small adjustments compound into sophisticated typography.
Neon Nostalgia
The 80s and 90s revisited

Neon colors dominated the 1980s. Bright yellows, magentas, and electric blues. Computer screens were just getting good, and designers wanted to show off.

By the 90s, this had evolved into web design. Bright GIFs. Comic Sans. Animated elements everywhere. Was it bad? Maybe. Was it fun? Absolutely.

Why neon matters

Neon colors communicate energy, youth, and rebellion. They're impractical but unforgettable. Use them when you need to grab attention.

Playful Experimentation
Breaking rules with intention

The best designers know the rules well enough to break them intelligently. They understand why certain choices work, which gives them permission to violate those rules for effect.

Playful design doesn't mean chaotic design. Even in the most experimental layouts, there's often an underlying grid. The experimentation sits on top of structure.

Experiment within systems. Choose a grid. Choose a color palette. Choose a font family. Then break those rules deliberately, not randomly.

Vintage Warmth
The enduring appeal of warm, earthy tones

Burnt orange. Mustard yellow. Deep browns. These colors recall 1970s design, but they never really go out of style. They feel warm, approachable, and timeless.

Why warm colors work

Warm colors are inviting. They slow you down. They encourage reading and contemplation. When combined with serif typefaces, they create nostalgia.

Modern design often defaults to cool grays and blues. Warm colors are a path to differentiation and humanity.
Bringing it forward

Use warm color palettes in editorial designs, long-form content, or anything where you want to encourage deeper engagement. Pair with generous whitespace and elegant serif typefaces for maximum effect.

The Wild Posters movement—known in Dutch as Wilde Plakken—emerged in the 1970s as a form of artistic activism in the Netherlands. Artists created striking, large-scale posters that challenged conventions and sparked conversation. What began as a grassroots effort by a small group of committed designers quickly evolved into a cultural phenomenon that would influence graphic design globally.

The term "wilde plakken" literally means "wild posters"—an apt description for work that rejected commercial constraints and gallery gatekeeping. Instead, these artists saw the streets themselves as the exhibition space, and the public as their true audience.

These works were often pasted directly onto walls and public spaces, making them accessible to everyone. They combined bold typography, striking color combinations, and provocative messaging. The urgency and directness of the medium—screen-printed posters applied to city infrastructure—created an undeniable presence.

Artists used humor, political commentary, and visual experimentation to arrest attention. Some posters were purely typographic, exploring the boundaries of letter-form and composition. Others incorporated figurative or abstract imagery, pushing the viewer to think critically about what they were seeing.

The movement represented a democratic approach to art—breaking down barriers between fine art and public communication. Each poster was a conversation starter, a challenge to norms, and a celebration of visual language's power to move and inspire people. Unlike gallery exhibitions that required tickets or cultural capital to access, these works existed in the everyday spaces where people lived, worked, and moved through their cities. The effectiveness of the medium wasn't measured in sales or critical reviews, but in the reaction of passersby—the double-takes, the photographs people took, the conversations sparked on streets and in homes.

Today, this movement reminds us that graphic design is more than decoration—it's activism, communication, and cultural commentary all at once. The Wilde Plakken artists understood that formal innovation matters, but only insofar as it serves a larger purpose: to reach people, to change minds, to create beauty in unexpected places.

Their legacy persists in street art, activist graphic design, and the work of any designer who believes that visual communication has the power to challenge power structures and create meaningful change. From political campaigns to social movements, from environmental awareness to LGBTQ+ advocacy, designers continue to follow the example set by these Dutch pioneers—taking their work to the streets, refusing to be confined, and trusting in the power of images to move the world.

Editorial Typography Study

Design Through Time

A meditation on how typography shapes meaning across three centuries of visual culture.

The evolution of typefaces tells the story of human expression. Each serif, each sans-serif, each experimental form carries the weight of its era.

Classical typography emphasized formality and structure. Gutenberg's press introduced rigid hierarchy. Fonts were tools of authority, not expression. The page was a grid of rigid order.

Modernism shattered this order. Constructivism, De Stijl, Bauhaus—these movements liberated typography from tradition. Diagonals crossed grids. Sans-serifs challenged centuries of serif dominance.

Typography is not just how things look. It's about how they feel and what they mean.

Today we stand at another crossroads. Digital type scales infinitely. Variable fonts adapt to context. The constraints that once defined good design have dissolved. And yet meaning persists. The reader still seeks clarity. The designer still seeks expression. These timeless tensions remain.

Three centuries of graphic design teach us this: the medium changes, but the fundamental goal remains unchanged. We are still trying to communicate. We are still trying to move people with marks on a surface.

Corporate Identity Corporate Identity
Part One
The Name as Object

A company name is not simply a word. It is the first and most persistent signal of intent—the thing that precedes the product, the service, the promise. Before there is a building or a reputation, there is a name that must do the work of both.

Designers who have spent careers in corporate identity understand this intuitively. The name arrives before the mark. It shapes what the mark can become. A hard-edged monosyllable suggests precision and technology. A flowing polysyllable hints at warmth, at process, at care.

On Letters and Legibility

The letterform is not neutral. Each typeface carries centuries of association, and when a corporation borrows from that history—consciously or not—it borrows the meaning too. Grotesques imply rationality. Transitional serifs carry authority. Slab serifs suggest permanence.

What makes naming so difficult is that language is not fixed. Words shift. Associations corrode or deepen with use. A name that felt modern in 1968 may feel institutional today—and institutional may be precisely what a new client needs.

Part Two
Systems and Symbols

The mark that follows a name must resolve the same tensions, but in purely visual terms. Where language gives the designer the friction of shared meaning, an abstract symbol offers freedom—and with freedom, the responsibility of invention without a net.

Each symbol must be not decoration appended to a name, but a structure capable of standing alone in any context: on the side of a truck, embossed in metal, reversed out of a dark field at small scale.

Durability and Change

The longest-lived corporate identities succeed not by anticipating every future context, but by possessing a clarity that survives them. Simplicity is not a style—it is a strategy against obsolescence. Complexity ages; reduction endures.

What we call a "timeless" identity is usually just one that was stripped, at the moment of its creation, of every element that could have anchored it to its own era. What remains is a residue of intent—an idea that was already asking to exist before any particular decade gave it permission.
